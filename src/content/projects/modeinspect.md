Hello! Charmayne here. 

This is how I think about this space and what excites me about your position in it. It’s based on external signals and my experience as both designer and GTM practitioner. 

I understand there is context that might change some of these views.

## 1. How growth works in design tooling

Adoption in design tooling is community-led and earned through usage.  The tool that designers love spreads. Figma proved this: three years in stealth, two years free, no sales team for years after that. 70% of enterprise deals started with a user already on a paid plan. The IC was always the champion; the buyer followed.

This extends beyond Figma. [Paper.design](http://Paper.design) (Accel-backed, AI-native) runs the same playbook. It extends into dev infrastructure: GitHub and Vercel both serve enterprises but both have generous free tiers. Every top-down enterprise sale in tooling where the end user has daily interaction still needs bottom-up pull to close and especially to renew.

<b>Trust in design tooling is earned through usage, not demos.</b> The product has to spread through the work itself.

## 2. What excites me about Modeinspect's position

I believe ModeInspect is building something that genuinely doesn't exist elsewhere right now: a tool that manages the transition for non-technical designers in the AI-native era. 

Most AI-native tools are asking designers to move closer to code. [Pencil.dev](http://Pencil.dev) positions itself as "Design Mode for Cursor," Paper renders everything as HTML/CSS.

Instead, you’re building for "code adapts to designers". I think it's a sharp product philosophy for the large population of professional designers at scale in companies who aren't going to become code-literate.

## 3. Tensions I'd want to explore

### Self-serve

ModeInspect's value is inseparable from organizational context: you need a real codebase, a real design system. That makes a pure product-led growth motion structurally harder than a blank-canvas tool. I understand this constraint.

But my instinct as a designer is that the current public demo **isn't enough** to create the emotional moment for the end user. A sandbox with sample data demonstrates capability, but the designer needs to feel the pain relief on their own work to become an internal champion.

I thought about workarounds. E.g. a "connect your own repo" free tier, especially given the rise of vibe-coding designers who deploy via GitHub (hi, that's me!). 

But I keep hitting the same wall: designers who already have repos are technical enough to use Cursor or Claude Code directly. The value prop collapses for designers that could most easily self-serve into it.

Hard problem, and no copy-paste answer from the Figma playbook.

### Crazy idea:

What if ModeInspect offered a free integration for frontend engineers that lets them generate a shareable visual-editing link for any deployed branch or staging URL? 

Engineer points it at a PR, gets a link, sends it to designer: "make the visual changes you want, I'll get them back as code."

- Engineer = initial champion (adopts it to save themselves time)
- Designer = beneficiary (pulled in via link)
- Org = standardizes because both sides love it
- Viral mechanic = the link itself, like Figma's shared URL

Nothing on the market does this yet. [CSS Pro](https://csspro.com/) is the closest, but it only captures CSS overrides. ModeInspect already has the hard parts (DeepCode, sandbox, PR output). The gap is packaging a lightweight version as a self-serve tool engineers can adopt alone.

This is a hypothesis and positioning to stress-test, not a recommendation. I'd want to validate against customer feedback, technical feasibility, and your roadmap.

### Community

One area I'd want to invest in early is building community and content around the handoff problem itself, not just ModeInspect as a product. The pain of designs getting lost in translation is universal and emotional. Every designer has stories about it. 

Making ModeInspect the company that <b>owns</b> this conversation (content, events, resources)  builds <b>awareness and inbound</b> that feeds both self-serve and enterprise.

## 4. Where I'd start

Understand the current funnel deeply: where prospects come from, where they drop, what customers say about why they bought and why they stay.

Then,

1. **Build inbound demand, not just outbound pipeline.** If trust is earned through usage and the IC needs to be the champion, then the GTM motion has to generate inbound demand from those ICs, not just outbound pipeline from the buyer.
2. **Test channels aggressively.** Influencers, events, community (would suggest tech twitter/X, discords, reddit), partnerships, etc
3. **Sharpen positioning before messaging.** Looking at the site, the product speaks to multiple audiences today. I want to identify which has the strongest pull through customer conversations and cheap positioning tests, then build everything around that single story.
4. **Explore a self-serve product wedge if possible.** Build bottom-up demand without requiring full codebase onboarding. My bet is that a free lightweight tool targeting engineers as the entry point could be an unlock.

Looking forward to discussing these ideas together.
